Pili

A calm connection system for older adults, families and caregivers.

Pili helps older adults stay connected through simple everyday moments, photos, voice notes, quick replies, reminders, and reassurance, without requiring them to manage a complex device.

Timeline
March – May 2026
Role
UX Research, Product Design, UI Direction, Physical Prototyping
Team
Junchao & Niharika
Tools
Figma, 3D Printing, Physical Prototyping
UX Research Product Design UI Direction Physical Prototyping
Overview

What is Pili?

Pili is a calm connection system designed for older adults living apart from family, especially in care environments such as assisted living, nursing homes, and hospitals.

Pili combines three touchpoints: a tabletop device for older adults, a family app for sending moments, and a caregiver dashboard for care environments.

Pili Device

A tabletop device for older adults, with simple physical buttons, calm messaging, and visible reassurance.

Family App

Lets family members send photos, notes, and quick check-ins without making the older adult manage another app.

Caregiver Dashboard

Helps care staff understand resident status, support requests, and room-level information.

The Challenge

The Problem

Regular contact does not always mean emotional closeness. For many older adults, communication tools can feel overwhelming, and asking for support can feel difficult or disruptive.

The Why

Why it Matters

The issue is not only loneliness. It is the gap between wanting to feel connected and having tools that feel simple, calm, and appropriate for older adults, families, and caregivers.

Research Findings

User Needs

  1. 01

    Feel remembered without having to ask for attention

  2. 02

    Receive family updates without navigating complex apps

  3. 03

    Ask for support without feeling watched or monitored

  4. 04

    Trust the device through simple, familiar interaction

Digital Interfaces

Designing the Digital Touchpoints

While the physical device reduces interaction complexity for the older adult, the supporting apps help family members and caregivers send care, monitor updates, and respond when needed.

Family App

The family app helps relatives send photos, short messages, and quick emotional check-ins to the older adult’s Pili device.

Send PhotoSend MessageSend “Thinking of You”Message History

Caregiver Dashboard

The caregiver dashboard helps staff view resident profiles, care requests, and room-level information without turning the system into a surveillance tool.

Resident OverviewCare RequestsRoom InfoQuick Access
Physical Making

Form Exploration

I explored multiple physical directions before arriving at a calm, visible, approachable tabletop device.

The final form needed to feel stable, familiar, and easy to understand. I tested circular companion objects, softer rounded forms, and finally a sloped tabletop direction with a screen, physical buttons, and a grounded base.

Version 1

Circular companion form

A soft, familiar object language inspired by clocks, speakers, and tabletop objects.

Version 2

Softer rounded object

A more minimal form with fewer visual cues and a calmer silhouette.

Version 3

Final direction

A sloped tabletop form with a clear screen angle, large physical buttons, and a stable base.

SCAD Senior Showcase · 2026

Exhibited at the SCAD Senior Show

Pili was presented at the SCAD senior showcase as a complete product and experience system. The exhibit included the physical prototype, interaction cards, app touchpoints, and process documentation.

Outcome

Outcome

Pili became a complete connection system spanning physical product design, mobile UI, caregiver tools, prototyping, and exhibition storytelling.

The project helped me practice designing across emotional needs, accessibility, physical interaction, and service ecosystems.

Design Your Lab

A planning tool designed for a BioSpherix mechanical engineer to turn client requirements into configurable 2D system layouts before high-effort CAD or Blender work begins.

Nihar · Mechanical Engineer at BioSpherix
My role

Timeline

Output

01 / The Person

Designed for one real workflow.

Nihar is a mechanical engineer at BioSpherix who translates client requirements into custom controlled-environment system layouts. Before production-level modeling begins, he needs to understand what components belong in the system, how they fit within the available footprint, and whether the configuration is viable.

The opportunity was not to replace engineering judgment. It was to give it a faster starting point.

The Problem

The problem wasn’t CAD. It was getting to CAD.

Early planning depended on manually interpreting client requirements, referencing scattered component information, and committing to high-effort modeling before fundamental layout decisions were settled. Changes in footprint, equipment, or configuration could mean repeated revisions before the design was ready for production.

Client Requirements ↓ Manual Interpretation ↓ CAD / Blender Model ↓ Client Change ↓ Rebuild + Rerender
Early Product Direction

Making the planning problem tangible.

Before building the full planner, I explored how the workflow could become a clearer, more visual planning experience: one that made components, space, and system information easier to understand before high-effort modeling began.

02 / Design Requirements

What would actually help?

REQ 01

Select real components

Choose chambers, controllers, and supporting equipment from a structured library.

REQ 02

Test configurations visually

Place components on a scaled 2D planning surface before committing to CAD.

REQ 03

See the system update

Track footprint, chamber count, gas requirements, weight, and pricing as the configuration changes.

REQ 04

Carry the work forward

Turn the configured system into a printable proposal for early client communication.

05 / AI-Assisted Build

AI accelerated the build. The workflow defined the product.

Research with Nihar shaped the problem. His feedback shaped the requirements. I used AI-assisted coding to prototype, test, and iterate the interface quickly while keeping product decisions grounded in the real workflow.

Human Research → Design Decisions → AI-Assisted Implementation
Outcome

A clearer starting point for a complex workflow.

Design Your Lab became a functional planning prototype that gives a mechanical engineer a clearer way to move from client requirements to system configuration and proposal output before committing to production-level CAD or Blender work.

The project taught me how to turn one person’s specialized workflow into a usable product system, use feedback as functional requirements, and direct AI toward implementation without letting it define the problem.

Wisdom ATL Eyewear

A three-frame eyewear, packaging, visual merchandising, and retail storytelling system.

This collaboration translated Wisdom ATL’s surreal, playful, and premium brand language into a buyer-facing eyewear system spanning frame families, physical prototyping, packaging, campaign thinking, merchandising, and lookbook direction.

Project Type
Product, Packaging & Visual Merchandising
Timeline
2026
Role
Product Concepting, Packaging Design, Visual Merchandising, Retail Pitch
Tools
Figma, Adobe Suite, 3D Visualization, Physical Prototyping
Overview

The collaboration

Wisdom ATL is an Atlanta-based artwear brand known for surreal design, cultural nuance, and a balance between stoic identity and childlike attitude. The project asked us to extend that world into eyewear, packaging, merchandising, and retail storytelling.

Challenge

Bold, but retail-ready

Create eyewear concepts that felt bold enough to stand apart, but polished enough to exist in a premium retail setting.

My Contribution

Product to buyer pitch

I contributed to product concepting, packaging directions, physical prototyping, visual merchandising, and the buyer-facing lookbook system.

Product System

Three frame families

The final system organized Wisdom’s eyewear into three retail roles: a flagship statement frame, a sport-driven guard frame, and a more refined everyday frame.

Statement / Face Jewelry

WSDM ONE

A flagship shield frame where the Wisdom wordmark becomes part of the structure. Designed as a statement piece that reads closer to wearable art than traditional eyewear.

Sport / Guard Structure

ARQ Sports / Helmet Frame RB

A sport-driven frame inspired by football helmet language, protective rails, wraparound coverage, and bold colorway energy.

Everyday / Premium

ARQ Daily / Helmet Frame QB

A more restrained frame family that translates the same helmet-inspired DNA into a softer everyday silhouette.

Strategy

Finding Wisdom’s retail position

The brand strategy explored how Wisdom could sit between expressive fashion eyewear and restrained premium frames. That position shaped the form language, material cues, colorways, packaging, and merchandising system.

Process

From visual language to wearable form

The frame development moved between logo-as-structure, shield silhouettes, helmet-inspired guard details, and physical tests. The goal was to make the frames feel like brand objects, not generic sunglasses.

Retail System

Packaging and retail world

The eyewear system expanded into packaging, collector-style cases, social moments, and display concepts that could support a buyer-facing pitch.

Campaign System

Lookbook and campaign direction

The final lookbook brought the product system together through frame introductions, colorways, MSRP and wholesale pricing, moodboards, social strategy, influencer strategy, reel storyboards, packaging, and merchandising.

WISDOM
Final Presentation

From lookbook to client presentation

The final phase of the Wisdom ATL collaboration brought the eyewear system into a client-facing presentation. Teams presented printed lookbooks, physical prototypes, packaging directions, and campaign systems to Wish ATL, showing how the work could live as both a product line and a retail story.

Outcome

Outcome

The project resulted in a retail-facing eyewear system spanning brand strategy, three frame families, physical prototyping, packaging directions, visual merchandising concepts, campaign thinking, lookbook design, and buyer pitch materials.

Behind the Build

I didn’t want to make another portfolio template. I wanted to make a small part of the internet that felt like me.

This site became a design project of its own: a record of visual decisions, AI conversations, rejected directions, system architecture, code, commits, and the process of turning organized chaos into something clear, playful, and intentional.

00 / The Brief

Not another portfolio template.

I needed the portfolio to do two things at once: make my work easy for recruiters to understand and still feel unmistakably personal. I wanted it to reflect the way I design, turning complexity into something clear, calm, playful, and intentional.

The challenge was not simply building a website. It was finding the balance between personality and usability, multidisciplinary range and clarity, AI assistance and human creative control.

01Recruiter-friendly
02Personal, not performative
03Multidisciplinary without feeling scattered
04Playful without becoming childish
05Built with AI, directed by a human
01 / Visual Evolution

The visual direction had to be earned.

The first direction explored a digital scrapbook, desktop archive, and organized-chaos aesthetic. It was playful, but over time the system became too card-heavy, too shadow-heavy, and too visibly AI-generated. The personality was being added through decoration rather than built into the design itself.

Initial Direction

Digital scrapbook Desktop archive Folder cards Tape Doodles Organized chaos

↓
What Didn’t Work
  • Too many cards.
  • Too many shadows.
  • Too AI-generated.
  • Too controlled.
  • Playful copy on a generic structure.
↓
Final Direction
  • Warm paper
  • Subtle grid
  • Strong color blocking
  • Editorial composition
  • Small moments of imperfection
  • Clear recruiter-facing UX

The goal was never to make the portfolio messy. It was to make it feel alive.

02 / AI Direction Log

AI generated options. I directed the system.

I used AI throughout the project for implementation, iteration, debugging, layout exploration, and critique. But the workflow was never prompt → output → accept. The actual process was a loop of direction, judgment, resistance, and revision.

Prompt → AI Output
↓
Human Critique
↓
Redirection → Revision → Decision

The value was not asking AI to design for me. It was learning how to articulate what felt wrong, define what better meant, and keep creative control through iteration.

03 / Records of Resistance

Technically correct. Creatively wrong.

I kept a record of moments where AI produced something functional, polished, or plausible, but wrong for the project. Those moments became a way of documenting taste, judgment, and creative resistance.

01Too Shadow-Heavy The output looked polished, but the visual language felt generic and overly generated. Rejected
02Too Many Cards Every idea was being placed inside another rounded rectangle. Simplified
03Playful Copy on a Boring Layout The words had personality. The design did not. Redirected
04Scrapbook Becoming Childish The organized-chaos idea was pushed too far. Pulled Back
05AI Inventing Meaning for an Image It Hadn’t Inspected Specific visual claims were being made without visual evidence. Corrected
06Decoration Before Structure Personality was being added through stickers, shadows, and effects instead of hierarchy and composition. Rebuilt

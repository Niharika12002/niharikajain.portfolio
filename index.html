Senior Capstone · UX / Product Design

Pili — Postpartum Care

A care companion app designed for new mothers navigating the emotional and physical complexity of postpartum life — built around the mothers who are always "fine" until they're not.

Role
Lead UX + Visual Designer
Type
Senior Capstone, SCAD
Platform
iOS Mobile App
Tools
Figma, Miro, Primary Research
🌱 pili-device.jpg
Overview
The Problem Nobody Talks About

The first year after birth is a medical event. But the healthcare system largely treats it like a footnote — six-week checkups, general advice, and the assumption that "new mom instinct" fills the gaps.

Pili starts from a different question: what does a new mother actually need day-to-day, emotionally and physically, when every resource she finds is either alarmist, oversimplified, or written for someone else?

The Gap
Between the Discharge Sheet and the First Pediatrician Visit

After delivery, most mothers receive a two-page discharge pamphlet and a six-week follow-up date. The gap in between — filled with sleep deprivation, hormonal shifts, physical recovery, feeding challenges, and the invisible labor of keeping another human alive — is largely unsupported.

Existing apps either track baby milestones or monitor postpartum depression symptoms. Pili lives in between: a daily care companion that holds both the mother and the baby in view at once.

📋 pili-problem-map.jpg
Research
What I Found
1 in 5
new mothers experience postpartum mood disorders — most go undiagnosed beyond the first few weeks
"Fine."
The most common answer when a new mother is asked how she's doing — even when she isn't
6 wks
The standard follow-up window after birth — the entire fourth trimester fits in this gap with almost no clinical touchpoints
3 AM
The hour most postpartum anxiety spirals happen. No support exists that meets someone there without judgment
User Research
Who Pili Is For

I conducted in-depth interviews with new and recent mothers, along with secondary research into postpartum clinical literature and support-seeking behavior online. The interviews were semi-structured and focused on daily routines, moments of overwhelm, and what "support" actually meant to them.

The pattern that kept surfacing: the biggest barrier wasn't access to information. It was permission — the permission to have needs of their own, separate from their baby's needs.

Key Research Finding
"Every resource I found was about the baby, or about whether I had postpartum depression. Nothing was just... for me, as a person who was going through something really hard and needed to feel less alone." — Interview participant
🗺️ pili-app-screens.jpg
Design Principles
Four Things Pili Refuses to Do
01
Never symptom-check without consent
Pili checks in — it doesn't survey. Emotional tracking is always opt-in and framed as care, not clinical measurement.
02
Never compete with the baby
Pili holds both the mother and the baby in view. Baby milestones and maternal care are woven together, not siloed.
03
Never alarm without context
Health information is presented with emotional context — not as a list of warning signs, but as a conversation.
04
Never assume "fine" means fine
The app's daily check-in is designed around what the mother wants to share, not what the system needs to know.
System Design
How Pili Works

Pili's core system is a daily care loop: a gentle check-in, a curated content feed, a symptom and mood journal, and an escalation pathway to professional care. Everything is designed to feel like a trusted friend — not a medical form.

1
Daily Check-in: A low-friction, high-empathy prompt that asks how the mother is feeling — physically and emotionally — with optional depth.
2
Care Feed: Personalized content — articles, prompts, gentle exercises — surfaced based on where she is in her postpartum timeline, not generic new-mom content.
3
Journal + Patterns: An optional mood and symptom log that builds a private record she can share with her provider at the six-week visit — or sooner.
4
Support Pathways: If a check-in surfaces something that needs more, Pili gently connects her to resources — peer support, crisis lines, or her care team — without alarming her.
📱 pili-screens-onboarding.jpg
📊 pili-screens-checkin.jpg
Reflection

Pili taught me that the hardest design challenge isn't building something complex — it's building something that doesn't add more weight to someone already carrying a lot. Every interaction had to earn its place. Every prompt had to ask only what it needed to. The visual system had to feel warm without being saccharine, clinical without being cold. Getting that balance right — that's what I kept coming back to.

BioSpherix — Lab Environment Planning

Redesigning the lab environment configuration workflow for research scientists who were spending hours juggling datasheets, PDFs, and spreadsheets to answer one question: will this setup work?

Role
UX Designer + Developer
Type
Live Web Application
Platform
Browser / Desktop
Tools
Figma, React, AI APIs
🧬 biospherix-interface.jpg
Context
The Researcher's Workflow Problem

BioSpherix makes precision laboratory enclosures — systems that control oxygen, CO2, temperature, and humidity for sensitive biological research. Configuring these systems requires researchers to cross-reference multiple product datasheets, understand compatibility rules, and validate configurations before purchasing.

The existing process was entirely manual. Researchers downloaded PDFs, built their own spreadsheets, emailed sales reps with questions, and often discovered incompatibilities only after weeks of back-and-forth.

3–5 hrs
Typical time a researcher spends manually configuring a lab setup before reaching out to sales
40%
Of initial configurations submitted to sales had at least one compatibility issue requiring revision
2+ weeks
Average sales cycle just to validate a configuration — before any purchasing decision
Solution
An AI-Assisted Configuration Tool

I designed and built a web application that guides researchers through the configuration process — letting them build a lab setup step by step, with real-time compatibility checking and AI-assisted recommendations.

The AI layer isn't a chatbot. It's embedded intelligence: as the user makes selections, the system surfaces compatibility warnings, suggests common configurations for their research context, and explains why certain combinations do or don't work.

⚙️ biospherix-configurator.jpg
Key Features
What the Tool Does
Step-by-Step Builder
Guided configuration flow that mirrors how researchers actually think about their lab setup — by research context, not product catalog.
Real-Time Validation
Compatibility rules run automatically in the background. Errors surface before they become expensive mistakes.
AI Recommendations
Based on the researcher's stated context — cell type, protocol, lab size — the system suggests proven configurations from BioSpherix's library.
Design Process
From Spreadsheet to Interface

The design challenge wasn't making the interface look good — it was making the complexity legible. Researchers are domain experts. They know what an OxyCycler is. The interface couldn't talk down to them, but it also couldn't just be a form.

I mapped the existing spreadsheet logic, extracted the decision tree, and designed an interface that walked through the same decisions in a sequence that felt natural rather than algorithmic.

📐 biospherix-wireframes.jpg
✅ biospherix-validation.jpg
Reflection

BioSpherix was where I learned that good UX for expert users means getting out of the way. The temptation is to over-explain, to add tooltips on every button, to make the system feel "helpful." The researchers I was designing for didn't need handholding — they needed their existing knowledge made more powerful. That shift changed how I approach every technical interface design since.

SCADpro · Service Design · Journey Mapping

FIFA World Cup 2026 — Atlanta Fan Experience

A service design project for the 2026 FIFA World Cup in Atlanta — designing the fan experience at city scale, from arrival to atmosphere to the final whistle.

Context
SCADpro Industry Project
Type
Service Design / Experience Strategy
Scale
City-wide, 800,000+ visitors
Tools
Miro, Figma, Journey Mapping
⚽ fifa-wayfinding.jpg
The Challenge
A City That Wasn't Built for This

Atlanta is hosting multiple World Cup 2026 matches at Mercedes-Benz Stadium. The stadium can hold 75,000 people. The surrounding infrastructure — transit, wayfinding, hospitality — was not designed around an influx of international fans who may not speak English, may be unfamiliar with Atlanta's geography, and are expecting a world-class experience.

The project challenged us to map the full fan journey — arrival at the airport, navigation to the stadium, the match experience itself, and the post-match exit — and identify where the experience breaks down and how to fix it.

🗺️ fifa-atlanta-map.jpg
Stakeholders
Who This Design Serves
Fans
Domestic and international visitors, many arriving in Atlanta for the first time — with different languages, navigation needs, and accessibility requirements
Operators
Stadium staff, transit workers, Atlanta police, volunteers — all needing to coordinate across a city-scale event
City
The City of Atlanta and the Atlanta Convention & Visitors Bureau — invested in the economic and reputational impact of the event
Journey Mapping
From Hartsfield-Jackson to Full Time

We built a comprehensive journey map covering five phases of the fan experience: arrival (airport), orientation (navigating the city), approach (getting to the stadium), the match, and departure. Each phase was analyzed for pain points, moments of delight, and systemic failures.

The most critical finding: wayfinding breaks down completely between the MARTA station and the stadium entrance — a half-mile stretch that lacks clear signage, shade, or any indication that fans are heading in the right direction.

📍 fifa-journey-map-full.jpg
Design Directions
What We Recommended
Wayfinding System
A ground-level wayfinding language — color-coded by language and fan zone — that works even in a crowd, even without a phone signal.
Fan Zone Anchors
Designated gathering points across downtown Atlanta that create community and distribute crowd load before and after matches.
Digital + Physical Bridge
An official event app layer that layers over MARTA's existing system — real-time crowd guidance, language support, and emergency routing.
Reflection

FIFA was the first time I designed at a scale where every decision had to account for thousands of people with completely different contexts. You can't design a "default user" when your user base is one person from Senegal, one family from Brazil, and one group of college students from Georgia, all arriving at the same gate at the same time. That shift from persona-thinking to systems-thinking was the biggest thing I took away.

Wisdom ATL Eyewear — Brand Identity

A complete brand identity and retail packaging system for an Atlanta-born independent eyewear brand — from logo to lens cloth, shelf to shop window.

Role
Brand Designer
Deliverables
Identity, Packaging, Retail Display
Market
Independent Eyewear / Atlanta
Tools
Figma, Illustrator, InDesign
👓 wisdom-packaging.jpg
Brand Context
An Atlanta Brand That Thinks Like a Local

Wisdom ATL is an independent eyewear brand rooted in Atlanta's creative culture — distinct from national chains, and designed for customers who want glasses that feel like a choice, not a default. The brief called for an identity that was sophisticated without being cold, local without being provincial, and bold without being loud.

Atlanta's creative scene — its music, its visual arts community, its architecture — became the research ground for the visual direction. The brand had to feel like it belonged here.

🎨 wisdom-moodboard.jpg
Packaging System
Every Touchpoint Considered

The packaging system spans the full retail and unboxing experience: primary case, secondary box, lens cloth, care card, and shopping bag. Each item is a considered brand moment — consistent in material and tone, distinct enough to reward close attention.

Case
Hard case with embossed wordmark — designed to be kept, not discarded
Box
Secondary retail box with interior print — the unboxing moment as a brand statement
Care Card
Small-format brand card that uses the brand voice: specific, warm, distinctly ATL
Retail
Visual merchandising direction for independent retail — how the brand looks on a shelf, not just in print
📦 wisdom-case-detail.jpg
🏪 wisdom-retail-display.jpg
Reflection

Wisdom ATL taught me how much meaning lives in material choices — the weight of a box, the texture of a lens cloth, the decision to emboss rather than print. Good brand packaging design isn't decoration. It's the brand's handshake with the customer at the moment they decide this thing was worth buying. Every choice either earns that moment or wastes it.

Naagin Sauce — Campaign Design

Campaign and marketing design for Naagin Sauce, an unapologetically Indian hot sauce brand that wears its identity loud and refuses to apologize for it.

Role
Graphic Designer
Type
Campaign / Marketing Design
Brand
Naagin Sauce (India)
Tools
Illustrator, Photoshop, InDesign
🌶️ naagin-campaign.jpg
The Brand
Hot Sauce That Knows Exactly What It Is

Naagin is not trying to be Tabasco. It's not trying to be a "fusion" brand. It's Indian hot sauce, full stop — bold flavors, bold personality, zero apology. The brand's existing visual language is already charged: the Naagin (serpent goddess) iconography, the bright India-ink red, the graphic density of Indian poster culture.

My role was to extend that language into campaign contexts — social content, print, promotional materials — while keeping the brand's loud energy intact.

🐍 naagin-brand-system.jpg
What the Work Shows
Campaign Design in Practice

The campaign work demonstrates my ability to work within and extend a strong existing visual identity — understanding what a brand is trying to say, then finding the most direct way to say it louder. With Naagin, "louder" is always the right answer. The challenge is staying in control of loud.

Design Context
Campaign materials were designed across social formats (Instagram, packaging inserts, print ads) with a focus on maintaining graphic consistency across contexts that reward high visual contrast and cultural specificity.
📱 naagin-social-01.jpg
🖼️ naagin-print-01.jpg
📦 naagin-packaging-01.jpg
Reflection

Naagin was a reminder that working with a brand that's already visually confident is its own kind of challenge. You can't start from scratch. You can't redesign it to feel more "you." Your job is to understand it deeply enough to extend it — to make something new that still sounds like the same voice. That discipline — extension over invention — is a skill I keep coming back to.

KUAI — AI Fashion & Lifestyle Planner

A concept AI app that thinks alongside you about your wardrobe, your plans, and how the two connect — exploring what it looks like when fashion planning becomes genuinely intelligent.

Role
UX Designer / Concept Lead
Type
AI UX Concept / Prototype
Platform
iOS Concept App
Tools
Figma, Prototyping, Concept Design
✨ kuai-app-screens.jpg
The Concept
What If Your Wardrobe Thought With You?

KUAI starts with a frustration most people recognize: you have an event, you have a wardrobe full of clothes, and somehow you still feel like you have nothing to wear. The problem isn't the clothes — it's the context gap between what you own and what you need.

KUAI bridges that gap with an AI layer that understands your wardrobe, your calendar, your preferences, and your budget — and surfaces the right outfit for the right moment without the decision fatigue.

App Structure
Four Core Modules
Closet
Your wardrobe as a living inventory — photographed, tagged, and understood by the system
Plan
Calendar-connected outfit planning — KUAI sees what's coming and prepares options in advance
Shop
AI-filtered shopping recommendations that fill actual gaps in your wardrobe, not just trending items
Style
A personal style profile that evolves over time — learning what you reach for and what stays on the hanger
👗 kuai-closet-view.jpg
📅 kuai-plan-view.jpg
AI UX Considerations
Where AI Earns Its Place

KUAI raised questions I kept returning to throughout the design: when does AI assistance feel like help versus surveillance? The closet inventory model requires the user to trust the system with something personal — their clothes, their calendar, their habits. That trust has to be earned through transparency and control, not assumed.

Every AI recommendation in KUAI shows its reasoning. You always know why a suggestion was made, and you can always override it. The system learns from your choices — but the choices are always yours.

Reflection

KUAI is where I got most interested in the ethics of AI UX — not in an abstract way, but in a very practical way. How do you make an AI feature feel useful rather than creepy? How do you communicate model confidence without creating false certainty? How much does the user need to understand about how a recommendation was made to trust it? I don't have complete answers yet. But asking those questions more carefully has changed how I think about every AI-touched interface I design.
